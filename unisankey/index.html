Canada offers among the highest rates of government support for university-industry research collaboration (UIRC) in the world to encourage the commercialization of advanced technology. Granting agencies such as OCE are tasked with identifying and funding the collaboration projects that have the greatest likelihood of commercialization.

UIRCs are complex projects that involve several stakeholders: 1) the academic researchers that carry out much of the research work, 2) the collaborating firms that contribute cash and/or in-kind (expertise, staff time, equipment, etc.), 3) the universities that manage the academic researchers’ resources and aid in commercialization, and 4) the granting agencies like OCE that influence the size, length and scope of the UIRC projects through their program guidelines. Each of these stakeholders have unique and identifiable characteristics.

There is considerable debate about the effect that certain characteristics may have on the likelihood of commercialization of UIRC outcomes. For example, is commercialization more likely in projects involving newer, more junior researchers or older, more seasoned researchers? Are cash contributions or in-kind contributions from the collaborating firm a better indicator of the likelihood of commercialization? Is commercialization more likely in projects at smaller or larger universities?

In addition, different characteristics may be associated with specific types of commercial outcomes, such as 1) starting up new companies or 2) technology licenses to existing companies. For example, are some academic researchers more likely to generate startups? Do the policies and preferences of university technology transfer offices influence whether project outcomes are commercialized via license vs. startup? Does the size of a firm's contribution to a project indicate a greater likelihood that the firm will license the project's outcomes? Understanding the characteristics of successful UIRC projects could help practitioners like OCE Business Development staff focus their efforts on the types of projects most likely to generate the desired outcomes.

A recent study used data from OCE-funded UIRC projects over a 10 year period to explore whether projects with certain characteristics had a greater likelihood of commercial outcomes.
